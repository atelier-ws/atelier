[assistant] This is a decision where the default answer should be "probably not yet" — most monolith performance problems are solved without microservices, and microservices bring a large distributed-systems tax. Here's how I'd frame the factors.

## First: is this actually an architecture problem?

Slowness is rarely fixed by splitting services — you just distribute the same slow code across a network. Before anything, confirm you've profiled and ruled out the usual Django culprits:

- **N+1 queries** — the single most common Django perf killer. Check `select_related`/`prefetch_related`, use Django Debug Toolbar or `django-silk`.
- **Missing DB indexes** and slow queries — look at `pg_stat_statements`.
- **Synchronous work that should be async** — offload to Celery/RQ (email, image processing, third-party API calls).
- **No caching layer** — Redis/Memcached for expensive queries, template fragments, or per-view caching.
- **Under-provisioned resources** — sometimes it's just DB connection pooling (PgBouncer) or too few gunicorn workers.

If you haven't done these, do them first. They're days of work, not quarters, and often reclaim the performance you're chasing.

## If it's genuinely a scaling/organizational problem, the real factors

**1. Are your pain points actually about scale, or about team velocity?**
Microservices solve *organizational* scaling (many teams stepping on each other, independent deploy cadences) more than they solve raw performance. If it's one team and the app is just slow, microservices will make it *slower and harder*. Be honest about which problem you have.

**2. Can you find clean service boundaries?**
Look for bounded contexts with minimal shared data. If billing, notifications, and search each own distinct data and talk through narrow interfaces, they're candidates. If everything joins against a central `users`/`orders` table, you'll end up with a distributed monolith — the worst of both worlds (network latency *plus* tight coupling).

**3. The database is the hardest part.**
Splitting services means splitting data. You lose ACID transactions across boundaries and inherit eventual consistency, distributed transactions/sagas, and data duplication. In Django this is especially painful because the ORM, migrations, and admin assume a shared DB. This is usually where "let's do microservices" projects stall.

**4. Do you have the operational maturity?**
Microservices demand: service discovery, centralized logging/tracing (you can no longer read one stack trace), CI/CD per service, container orchestration, monitoring/alerting, and on-call for N services. If you don't have solid observability on the monolith today, you're not ready.

**5. Team size and Conway's Law.**
Service boundaries tend to mirror team boundaries. A common rule of thumb: you want roughly one team per service. A 5-person team running 12 services is a maintenance trap.

## The middle path most teams should take first

- **Modular monolith** — enforce internal module boundaries (Django apps with strict, one-directional dependencies; consider `import-linter` to enforce them). You get most of the organizational clarity with none of the network tax, and it's the natural staging ground for a later extraction.
- **Extract one service** — if you're serious, carve off *one* genuinely independent, high-value piece (often something async or CPU-bound like search, PDF generation, or a recommendation engine) and learn the operational cost before committing the whole app.
- **Scale the monolith horizontally** — Django is stateless per request; you can often run many instances behind a load balancer against a read-replica'd database for a long time.

## My recommendation

Start with profiling. In my experience the majority of "we need microservices" conversations are triggered by problems that a week of query optimization, caching, and moving work to Celery would resolve. If after that you still have distinct teams blocked on each other and clear bounded contexts, extract *one* service as an experiment rather than committing to a full split.
