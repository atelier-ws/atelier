[assistant] This is a classic decision, and the honest starting point is: **most "monolith is slow" problems are not solved by microservices.** Slowness is usually a performance problem; microservices are an organizational/scaling solution that often makes raw performance *worse* (network hops, serialization, distributed transactions). So the first factor is diagnosing what "slow" actually means.

## First: is this even a microservices problem?

Profile before you architect. Common Django slowness has cheap fixes that don't require splitting anything:

- **N+1 queries** — `select_related`/`prefetch_related`, `django-debug-toolbar` or `nplusone` to find them.
- **Missing DB indexes** / slow queries — check `pg_stat_statements`.
- **No caching layer** — Redis/Memcached for expensive reads.
- **Synchronous work that should be async** — offload to Celery (email, PDF gen, third-party API calls).
- **Undersized infrastructure** — sometimes it's just DB connection pooling (`pgbouncer`) or more web workers.

If you haven't exhausted these, do that first. A well-tuned monolith serves enormous scale.

## If it's genuinely an architecture/scaling problem, the key factors:

**1. Team & organizational structure (usually the real driver)**
Microservices pay off when multiple teams are stepping on each other in one codebase — merge conflicts, coupled deploy trains, one team's bug blocking another's release. If you're one team of 5, the coordination overhead of microservices will cost more than it saves. Conway's Law cuts both ways.

**2. Where are the actual bottlenecks / independent scaling needs?**
Is one part of the app (e.g. a reporting engine, image processing, a search feature) driving load with a profile totally different from the rest? That's a natural extraction candidate — you can scale it independently. If load is uniform, splitting buys you little.

**3. Domain boundaries — are they clear?**
Splitting along the wrong seams creates a "distributed monolith": all the network cost, none of the independence. You must have well-defined bounded contexts. If your models are a tangled web of foreign keys across the whole app, you don't understand your boundaries well enough to split yet.

**4. Data ownership and transactions**
This is the hardest part. A monolith gives you free ACID transactions across all data. Split it and you get distributed data — eventual consistency, sagas, no cross-service joins. Ask: which data can be cleanly owned by one service? If everything joins to everything, you're not ready.

**5. Operational maturity**
Microservices require infrastructure you may not have: service discovery, centralized logging/tracing, CI/CD per service, container orchestration, monitoring, on-call for N services. Do you have the platform/DevOps capacity? Debugging a distributed system is materially harder.

**6. Cost of the transition vs. cost of staying**
This is a multi-quarter effort that competes with feature work. Be honest about the opportunity cost.

## The pragmatic middle path

Before full microservices, consider:

- **Modular monolith** — enforce clean module boundaries *inside* the Django app (separate apps, no cross-module model imports, service layers). This gives you most of the organizational clarity, keeps deployment simple, and makes a future split far easier if you still need it. Often this is the actual answer.
- **Strangler fig extraction** — if you do split, extract *one* well-bounded service first (the clearest boundary with the strongest independent-scaling case), learn from it, and don't commit to rewriting everything.

## My recommendation

Ask the team three questions:
1. Have we profiled and fixed the obvious performance issues? (Usually the answer alone ends the debate.)
2. Is our pain actually *organizational* — teams blocked on each other — or just *technical* slowness?
3. Can we name three services with clean data ownership right now?

If you can't answer #3 concretely, the move is a **modular monolith** plus performance tuning, not microservices. Reach for microservices when the driver is independent team velocity or genuinely divergent scaling profiles — not raw latency.
